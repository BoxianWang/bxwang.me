MATHEMATICS

A crucial step in studying mathematics is understanding the relationship between different topics of mathematics. To that end I have compiled a list of worthwhile references that hopefully serve as introductions to each topic. This by no means is a complete guide, and most reviews could only be regarded as personaly comments. This page is more geared towards a personal comparison of various introductory texts. rather than objective judgements due to my lack of knowledge.

Analysis

Principles of Mathematical Analysis, Rudin

Great introductory text that introduces metric topology early on. Read only the first eight chapters, as there are better references for multivariate analysis.

Mathematical Analysis, Zorich

Another great introductory text. I hear that Volume II is especially good in its treatment of differential forms. Could be used as a complement to Rudin. I plan to use it for review.

Analysis on Manifolds, Munkres

An alternative to Spivak's Calculus on Manifolds, but more detailed. Good as a supplement for multivariate analysis and an introduction to manifold theory (which is significantly more involved that the case in Euclidean spaces).

Complex Analysis, Ahlfors

The classic complex analysis text.

Lectures Series in Analysis (Four Volumes), Stein

Stein's analysis lectures could be perfect references for second-year analysis, though some treatments may lack in rigor.

Real and Complex Analysis, Rudin

Another dictionary. Great for brushing up but not starting off.

Algebra

Abstract Algebra, Dummit and Foote

The classic introductory text disliked by many. I, for one, think some treatments are overly clumsy and lacking in insights, and algebra is all about insights.

Algebra, Hungerford

A very condensed Bourbaki-style textbook. My favorite by far.

Algebra, Lang

The classick "brick". A hugely rich reference which I have yet to appreciate.

Linear Algebra Done Right, Axler

A functional analysis take on linear algebra, though I would personally prefer a more algebraic treatment. Hoffman and Kunze would be an example of that.

Advanced Linear Algebra, Roman

A pretty nice second course in linera algebra by the look of it. Good for those who really want to figure out linear algebra. Otherwise the standard content in a functional analysis or algebra textbook would suffice.

Topology and Geometry

Topology, Munkres

Nice introductory text, the general topology part of which serves as a good companion to studying analysis.

Introduction to Smooth Manifolds, Lee

A comprehensive guide to differential geometry. I would probably start it after I finish Munkres or Spivak.

Other (More Advanced)

A Course in Arithmetic, Serre

A brilliant book in algebraic number theory.
